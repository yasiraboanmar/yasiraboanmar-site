Early in my HR career, I watched one organization promote an employee simply because "the manager liked him," and dismiss another because the manager "didn't like his attitude" — with no written criterion guiding either decision. In another organization, every promotion, dismissal, or salary increase passed through a clear standard, documentation, and review. The difference between the two wasn't budget or headcount. It was the presence — or absence — of **governance**.
 
This article breaks down that difference from a practical lens, after 14 years of seeing both sides up close.
 
---
 
## Part One: The Core Distinction
 
**HR governance** refers to the framework that structures how human capital is managed within an organization — the policies, procedures, and structures that ensure fairness, transparency, and legal compliance, anchored in principles of ethics and accountability.
 
In simpler terms: **governed management** means a decision is built on a written standard known to every employee in advance. **Ungoverned management** means a decision is built on a manager's personal judgment — one that shifts from case to case, and sometimes from mood to mood.
 
The distinction isn't about "having policies on paper." Many organizations have a polished policy manual that sits unused while the organization is actually run on whim. Real governance is measured by one question: **Is what's written actually applied to everyone, without exception?**
 
---
 
## Part Two: Warning Signs of Ungoverned Management
 
From experience, these are the clearest signals that governance is missing:
 
### 1. Personal Decisions Instead of Criteria-Based Ones
A promotion is granted because the manager "trusts him," not because he hit defined performance indicators. A termination happens because the manager "got fed up," not because of a documented violation.
 
### 2. No Clear, Published Standards
There's no visible ladder: who qualifies for promotion? Based on which metrics? On what timeline? The employee doesn't know the answer because no one does — the rationale gets written *after* the decision, not before it.
 
### 3. Double Standards
The same violation gets one employee docked pay while a manager overlooks it entirely for another — depending purely on the personal relationship with each. This is the most dangerous sign, because it breaks trust in a way that's hard to repair.
 
### 4. Absence of Documentation
No meeting minutes, no written performance reviews, no documented rationale for decisions. When a dispute arises, there's no record protecting either the organization or the employee.
 
### 5. Power Concentrated in One Person
Every decision — from hiring to firing — passes through a single individual with no oversight or review committee, regardless of the organization's size.
 
---
 
## Part Three: The Pillars of Governed HR Management
 
### 1. Written Policies Communicated to Everyone
Drafting clear, comprehensive HR policies and communicating them to all employees — ensuring they actually understand them — is the first step. A policy the employee doesn't know about has no practical value.
 
### 2. Objective Promotion and Evaluation Criteria
A performance evaluation ladder tied to measurable KPIs, applied identically to every employee at the same job level.
 
### 3. Transparency in Compensation Structure
Known salary bands for each job grade, with clear criteria for raises and allowances — not hidden individual negotiations that create unjustified gaps between employees in the same role.
 
### 4. Periodic Policy Review
Policies aren't a static text. Regular reviews and updates are necessary to ensure they stay aligned with the organization's evolving needs and external changes.
 
### 5. An Accountability and Oversight Mechanism
A committee or independent reviewing body, separate from the direct manager, ensures sensitive decisions — terminations, senior promotions — aren't made by one person with no oversight.
 
---
 
## Part Four: Steps to Transition from Arbitrary to Governed Management
 
Transformation doesn't happen with a single decision — here are gradual, practical steps:
 
### Step 1: Diagnose the Current State
Review every impactful decision from the past year (promotions, terminations, raises) and ask: did each one have a prior written criterion? The answer reveals the real size of the gap.
 
### Step 2: Draft the Core Policies First
Don't try to govern everything at once. Start with the most sensitive areas: hiring policy, evaluation and promotion policy, disciplinary and termination policy.
 
### Step 3: Communicate Policies and Train Managers
A written policy without training for direct managers on how to apply it remains ink on paper. A manager accustomed to personal judgment needs time to adapt to a standard.
 
### Step 4: Build a Documentation System
Every sensitive decision is documented with its reasoning and reference point. This protects the organization legally and protects the employee from arbitrary treatment.
 
### Step 5: Establish an Oversight and Review Mechanism
Forming a dedicated HR governance committee with senior leaders and HR experts to oversee strategy development and implementation — even in a small organization, a review by someone independent of the initial decision-maker is enough.
 
### Step 6: Measure Impact and Continuously Improve
Use HR data — turnover rate, complaints, employee satisfaction survey results — to measure the impact of governance and refine it over time.
 
---
 
## Part Five: The Real Benefits of Governed Management
 
### Fairness as the Foundation of Trust
When employees know the same standard applies to everyone, trust in the organization grows measurably.
 
### Better Employee Retention
Organizations benefit from HR governance through stronger employee engagement and improved retention, because it guarantees fair treatment.
 
### Legal Protection for the Organization
In any labor dispute or complaint to a labor authority, a decision documented against a written standard protects the organization. A personal, undocumented decision exposes it to liability.
 
### Institutional Stability Independent of Individuals
When an organization relies on a system rather than a person, operations aren't disrupted when a manager leaves or resigns.
 
### Balancing Organizational and Employee Interests
HR governance aims to strengthen performance, efficiency, and the balance between organizational interests and employee interests.
 
---
 
## Part Six: The Real Drawbacks and Challenges
 
Governance isn't free — professional honesty requires naming the costs:
 
### Slower Decision-Making
Every decision must pass through criteria and approvals, which slows down the speed some managers are used to when "settling things on the spot."
 
### Occasional Rigidity
A rigid policy with no room for reasonable exceptions can become an obstacle in genuinely human or circumstantial cases that call for flexibility.
 
### Resistance to Change
Employees and managers may resist changes to how they're managed or governed, making it difficult to implement new policies — particularly among those accustomed to the influence of personal decision-making.
 
### Resource Cost
Implementing HR governance requires time, money, and specialized personnel, and some organizations may lack the resources to dedicate to this process.
 
### Poor Communication Undermines the Effort
Weak communication between management and employees can make it difficult to secure everyone's buy-in for governance procedures — even when the policies themselves are sound.
 
---
 
## Part Seven: The Impact on Fairness and Equality — The Heart of the Matter
 
This is where the real value of governance lies, and it's the point I consider most important after years in this field.
 
**Fairness without governance is mood. Fairness with governance is a system.** When a decision is left to personal judgment, "fairness" becomes hostage to the character of whoever holds power at that moment — which is unstable and cannot be relied upon at an organizational level.
 
Governance transforms fairness from a **volatile individual value** into a **fixed institutional system**:
 
- **Equal opportunity:** Every employee knows the promotion criteria in advance, so they can work toward it instead of relying on "winning the favor" of a specific person.
- **Equal treatment:** Same violation, same consequence — regardless of how close or distant the employee is from management.
- **Protection for those with less influence:** A new employee, or one less able to build personal relationships with management, gets the same opportunity as someone more socially connected — because the standard is the same for everyone.
- **Reduced unintentional discrimination:** The absence of a standard opens the door — even with good intentions — to bias based on personal impressions, nationality, or even appearance. A written standard closes that door.
In one sentence: **Governance is not an administrative luxury — it's the only guarantee that fairness survives after a good decision-maker leaves, and that injustice is prevented in the absence of oversight.**
 
---
 
## Practical Takeaways from 14 Years in HR
 
Every organization starts with personal-style management in its early phase — that's natural for small and startup companies. But the organization that succeeds and grows, while continuing to be run on mood, pays the price later: high turnover, labor disputes, and the loss of trust among top performers.
 
My practical advice: don't wait until the organization grows to start building governance. Starting early with simple, clear policies is far easier than trying to "correct" a management culture built on personal decision-making over years.
 
Governance doesn't mean losing human flexibility in management — it means that flexibility itself becomes a *declared standard*, not a mood-based exception granted to some employees and withheld from others.
