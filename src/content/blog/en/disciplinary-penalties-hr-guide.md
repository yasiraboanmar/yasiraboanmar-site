# How to Apply Disciplinary Penalties Without Legal Mistakes
 
Picture this: an employee is late for the first time in their entire career. Their direct manager immediately deducts a full day's wage — no written notice, no chance to explain. Weeks later, the employee files a labor complaint, and the company loses the case. Not because the employee was right to be late, but because the company got the **process** wrong.
 
This scenario repeats itself constantly across the private sector, and the root cause is almost always the same: treating the penalty schedule as a "menu of punishments" to choose from at will, when in reality it is a precise legal framework with strict procedural and substantive conditions. This article is written for HR professionals and employers — and for employees who want to understand their rights — to clarify how disciplinary penalties should actually be applied under Saudi Labor Law.
 
## Rule One: No Penalty Without a Legal Basis
 
Article 66 of the Labor Law limits the disciplinary penalties an employer may impose to exactly six — no more:
 
1. A written warning
2. A fine
3. Denial or postponement of a bonus/increment (for no more than one year)
4. Postponement of promotion (for no more than one year)
5. Suspension from work with loss of wages
6. Dismissal from work (only in the cases specified by law)
Article 67 is explicit on this point: **"An employer may not impose on an employee a penalty not provided for in this Law or in the work organization regulation."**
 
In practice, this means: denying an employee's annual leave, deducting from an end-of-service benefit, or issuing a "formal verbal reprimand" logged as an official penalty — all common practices in many organizations — are **legally void**, no matter how reasonable they may seem. They simply don't exist in the exhaustive list above.
 
## Rule Two: Due Process Before Any Penalty
 
This is where most HR practitioners go wrong. Articles 71 and 72 require a mandatory procedure before any penalty is imposed (except for a warning, or a fine capped at one day's wage for minor violations, where a verbal inquiry documented in a written record is sufficient):
 
1. **Written notification** to the employee of the alleged violation
2. **An inquiry**, giving the employee a chance to respond
3. **Documenting the employee's defense** in an official record
4. **Filing the record** in the employee's personnel file
5. **Issuing the decision in writing** and formally notifying the employee (by registered letter if they refuse to receive it or are absent)
6. **Informing the employee of their right to appeal** within 30 days to the designated authority within the company
Had this procedure been followed in our opening example, the company would have been on solid legal ground — regardless of the outcome of the decision itself.
 
## Rule Three: Timing Is Critical — Don't Delay, Don't Rush
 
The Law sets strict time limits that are frequently overlooked:
 
- **30-day investigation window (Article 69):** An employee cannot be accused of a violation discovered more than 30 days earlier, and no penalty may be imposed more than 30 days after the violation is established and the investigation concludes. Delaying the administrative decision forfeits the right to discipline.
- **180-day repetition window (Article 68):** A penalty cannot be escalated on the grounds of "repetition" if 180 days have passed since the previous violation without the employee having been formally notified in writing of the penalty for it.
The practical impact of that second point is significant: **repetition only counts if every prior violation was documented and formally communicated at the time.** If an employee was late in January and never received a written warning, and is late again in August, that second instance cannot legally be treated as the "second occurrence" under the schedule — because the first violation was never formally recorded as a precedent.
 
## Rule Four: Caps That Cannot Be Exceeded
 
- A fine for a single violation may not exceed **five days'** wages
- Total deductions in one month (all fines combined) may not exceed **five days'** wages
- Suspension without pay may not exceed **five days** in a month
- **More than one penalty** may not be imposed for the same violation
## The Model Schedule vs. a Company-Specific, Ministry-Approved Regulation
 
A point many HR professionals miss: the penalty schedule attached to the Executive Regulations is a **model, indicative schedule** issued by the Ministry — not the only legally binding text available.
 
The Law allows a company to draft its **own work organization regulation**, with a violation classification tailored to its specific operations — but on one essential condition: **it must be formally approved by the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development** (currently via the Qiwa platform). Without that approval, any internal penalty schedule a company drafts on its own has no legal standing, and the default reference before any judicial authority remains the model schedule attached to the Law.
 
**Practical recommendation:** Medium and large organizations — especially those with specialized operations such as logistics, warehousing, or manufacturing — should proactively draft a company-specific work regulation that reflects their actual operational risks (such as safety violations during shift work or equipment handling), then have it formally approved, rather than relying solely on the generic schedule, which may not cover every real-world scenario.
 
## A Complete Worked Example: The Case of "Ahmad"
 
Let's trace a hypothetical employee through two different tracks to show how progressive discipline actually works.
 
**Track One — Late Arrival (Working Hours Schedule, Item 1):**
 
| Occurrence | Violation | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Late by 10 minutes, no acceptable excuse | Written warning |
| 2nd | Repeated within the same contractual year | 5% deduction from daily wage |
| 3rd | Repeated again | 10% deduction |
| 4th | Repeated again | 20% deduction |
 
The schedule stops at the 4th occurrence with a 20% deduction — **it does not specify dismissal.** So what happens if Ahmad is late a fifth time? The company cannot improvise a harsher penalty on its own. The correct approach under the Law: issue an **explicit written warning** stating that continued lateness constitutes a serious breach of core duties, and that any further repetition will expose the employee to dismissal under Article 80 (breach of fundamental duties despite warning). Dismissal here is not automatic — it still requires a clear prior warning and a complete procedural record, just like any other penalty.
 
**Track Two — Behavioral Violation (Employee Conduct Schedule):**
 
Three months later, Ahmad gets into an altercation with a colleague at work. This is an **entirely different category** of violation (conduct, not attendance), so the count restarts at "first occurrence" under its own schedule: one day's wage deducted.
 
But if the violation were more severe — for example, verbal or physical aggression toward the responsible manager or a supervisor — the schedule allows **no gradual escalation at all**: the penalty from the **very first occurrence** is dismissal without notice or end-of-service benefit under Article 80. This illustrates an important principle: not every violation is subject to progressive discipline. Some justify immediate, lawful dismissal from the first instance — provided the full procedural requirements (notification, inquiry, documented defense, written record) are met.
 
## The HR Professional's Role in Ensuring Fairness
 
- **Consistency of standards:** The same violation must receive the same penalty for every employee, regardless of position or personal relationships
- **Separating detection from decision-making:** Ideally, the direct manager reports the violation, while the HR department or a committee makes the final decision — reducing the risk of retaliatory or impulsive rulings
- **Systematic documentation:** Use a standardized template for every case (date, description of the violation, employee's statement, decision) — even for a simple warning — since this documentation is exactly what future "repetition" calculations rely on
- **Periodic review of disciplinary records:** Before counting any violation as, say, the "fourth occurrence," verify that previous occurrences were properly documented, formally communicated, and fall within the correct 180-day window
- **Training direct managers** to never issue penalties themselves and to refer every case to HR first
## Common Mistakes That Cost Companies Labor Disputes
 
1. Skipping progressive discipline and jumping straight to dismissal for a first, minor violation
2. Imposing penalties not listed in the Law (such as deductions from end-of-service benefits or denying leave)
3. Exceeding the monthly deduction cap (five days' wages)
4. Lack of written documentation, making it difficult to prove "repetition" later before a court
5. Missing the 30-day window for investigation or for issuing the penalty
6. Applying an internal regulation that was never formally approved by the Ministry
7. Failing to inform the employee, in writing, of their right to appeal within 30 days
## Quick Checklist Before Signing Off on Any Penalty
 
- [ ] Is the penalty one of the six explicitly listed in the Law?
- [ ] Is the violation documented with a specific date, within 30 days of discovery?
- [ ] Was the employee notified in writing, given an inquiry, and their defense documented in a record?
- [ ] Was progressive discipline correctly applied based on the number of repetitions within the same contractual year and the 180-day window?
- [ ] Is the fine or deduction within the legal cap (5 days per violation, 5 days per month)?
- [ ] Was the decision issued in writing, and was the employee informed of their right to appeal within 30 days?
- [ ] If applying a non-model regulation, is it formally approved by the Ministry?
Applying disciplinary penalties is not routine administrative work — it is a legal responsibility that protects both the company and the employee. Strict adherence to both the form and substance of the Law is what preserves an organization's right to maintain order in the workplace, while safeguarding the dignity and rights the Law guarantees to every employee.
