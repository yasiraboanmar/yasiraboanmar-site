Overtime in Saudi workplaces is one of the most misunderstood topics in HR — 
whether it's employees unaware of their full entitlements, or employers 
unknowingly committing violations.

After 14 years in human resources, I'm sharing everything you need to know 
about overtime under Saudi Labor Law.



## What Is Overtime?

Overtime is any hour worked by an employee **beyond the official working hours** 
defined in their employment contract or the Saudi Labor Law.

Under **Article 98** of the Labor Law, employers may not require employees to 
work beyond their agreed hours except in specific circumstances — and when they 
do, overtime pay is legally mandatory.



## Standard Working Hours Under Saudi Labor Law

| Category | Daily Hours | Weekly Hours |
|---|---|---|
| Regular employees | 8 hours | 48 hours |
| Ramadan (Muslim employees) | 6 hours | 36 hours |

Any hour beyond these limits **constitutes overtime** and must be compensated 
accordingly.



## How Is Overtime Pay Calculated?

### Article 107 of the Labor Law:

An employee is entitled for each overtime hour to **one full hour's wage based 
on their total salary**, plus **50% of one hour's wage calculated on the basic 
salary only**.

**The Formula:**
**Practical Example:**

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Basic Salary | SAR 5,000 |
| Allowances | SAR 3,000 |
| Total Salary | SAR 8,000 |
| Hourly rate from total salary | SAR 33.33 (8,000 ÷ 30 ÷ 8) |
| 50% from basic salary | SAR 10.42 (5,000 ÷ 30 ÷ 8 × 50%) |
| **Overtime Hour Rate** | **SAR 43.75** |

**Summary:** Overtime hour = one full hour at total salary rate + half hour at 
basic salary rate.



## Working on Rest Days and Public Holidays

This is where most employers make their biggest mistakes.

**1. Working on the Weekly Rest Day (usually Friday)**
The employee is entitled to a substitute day off or a full day's additional 
pay, whichever is agreed upon.

**2. Working on Official Public Holidays (Eid, National Day, etc.)**
The employee is entitled to double pay for hours worked on these days.

**3. Working Beyond 6 Hours in Ramadan**
Hours exceeding 6 daily during Ramadan are calculated as overtime using the 
same formula above.



## Maximum Overtime Hours

Under Saudi Labor Law, employees may not be required to work more than 
**10 overtime hours per week** under normal circumstances, except in cases of 
extreme necessity as defined by the law.

Exceeding this limit without authorization exposes the employer to violations 
from the **Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development**.



## Common Mistakes Employers Make

**❌ Applying the 50% on the total salary instead of the basic salary**
The law is clear: the additional 50% is calculated on the basic salary only. 
It can be applied on a broader base (including some allowances) as long as the 
result is not less than what the basic salary would yield — the minimum 
benchmark is always the basic salary.

**❌ Calculating overtime on basic salary only, ignoring total salary**
Equally common — and equally wrong. The base hour (1:1) must be calculated 
from the total salary, not just the basic.

**❌ Replacing overtime with a fixed monthly allowance**
Paying a fixed "overtime allowance" without tracking actual hours may be a 
violation — unless that allowance was calculated in full compliance with the law 
from the start.

**❌ Failing to document overtime hours**
In any dispute, the burden of proof falls on the employer. No records means 
no defense in labor courts.

**❌ Overlooking Ramadan overtime**
Many organizations forget that working hours are reduced during Ramadan — any 
time beyond 6 daily hours must be treated and compensated as overtime.



## Employee Rights: Can They Refuse Overtime?

**Yes, under normal circumstances.** Employees cannot be forced to work 
overtime except in exceptional situations the law specifies, such as:

- Preventing accidents or repairing resulting damages
- Handling unexpected emergency conditions

Outside these cases, overtime requires the **employee's consent**.



## How to Manage Overtime in Your Organization

From practical experience, here are the best practices:

**1. Write a clear overtime policy**
Define the request process, who approves it, monthly caps, and how it's paid.

**2. Document every hour**
Whether through a time-attendance system or an HR platform — proper records 
protect both you and your employees legally.

**3. Pay overtime with the monthly salary**
Don't let overtime accumulate for months — delayed payments create disputes 
and erode trust.

**4. Monitor monthly trends**
Consistently high overtime hours signal a staffing shortage. Chronic overtime 
is not a solution — it's a symptom of a deeper problem.



## Conclusion

Overtime is not just a number added to payroll — it's a legal and 
administrative responsibility that requires a clear policy, accurate 
documentation, and correct calculation.

Understanding Article 107 of the Saudi Labor Law and applying the right formula 
protects your organization from violations and builds genuine trust with your 
employees.
