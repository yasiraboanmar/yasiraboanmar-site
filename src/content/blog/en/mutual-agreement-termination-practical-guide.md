Picture this: a short meeting with your manager, some vague talk about "the situation" and "what's next," and by the end he suggests you "wrap this up amicably, better for both sides." You nod, you agree, and you walk out of the office not entirely sure what just happened.
 
This scene plays out in offices everywhere, and the difference between an employee who walks away with full rights and one who quietly loses part of them almost always comes down to details no one explains to you in that meeting.
 
Based on handling dozens of similar cases, here's a practical guide — not legal text, but actual steps.
 
---
 
## The Real Difference Between "Mutual Agreement" and a Regular Resignation
 
In two simple lines:
 
- **Regular resignation (Article 75):** you alone decide to leave, and you owe a notice period.
- **Mutual agreement termination (Article 74):** both sides agree together to end the relationship, and the notice period and other mutual obligations fall away — but only through a written agreement between the two parties.
The problem isn't in the wording of the law. The problem is that most "mutual agreements" start **verbally**, and the employee is later asked to sign a document that may not exactly match what was agreed in conversation.
 
---
 
## 5 Practical Steps Before You Sign or Verbally Agree to Anything
 
### 1. Don't agree in the same meeting
No matter how gentle the pressure feels, say: "I appreciate this, I need a day or two to think it through." That's your right, and no regulation forces an immediate answer.
 
### 2. Ask for everything in writing
Any verbal agreement about the end date, entitlements, or extra terms — ask for it by email or a formal document. The right phrase: "That sounds good, could you send me the details by email so I can review them properly?"
 
### 3. Confirm the Qiwa documentation happens before you sign, not after
Don't accept "we'll register it later." Ask for the agreement to be documented on the Qiwa platform **at the same time** as signing, not days afterward.
 
### 4. Calculate your own entitlements beforehand
Before any meeting, know your number: end-of-service benefit, remaining leave balance, and any other financial dues. When you already have a clear figure in mind, it's much harder for anyone to slip in an agreement below what you're actually owed without you noticing.
 
### 5. Don't tie "mutual agreement" to unwritten future promises
Things like "we'll give you a great reference letter" or "we'll make your move to the new company easier" — if these promises are part of your decision, ask for them in writing. Otherwise they carry no legal weight, no matter how sincerely they were said.
 
---
 
## Warning Signs Worth Watching For
 
- **Unjustified urgency** — if there's pressure to close the matter "today," that's a signal to slow down, not speed up.
- **No documentation a week after the "verbal agreement"** — which means, legally, the agreement doesn't exist yet.
- **A mismatch between what was said verbally and what's in the written document** — never sign a text that differs from the actual agreement, even if you're told "it's just a formality."
---
 
## If the Agreement Stalls or Gets Ignored
 
If a reasonable period passes (one to two weeks) with no formal documentation or written response, you have the option to submit a **formal termination or non-renewal notice under Article 74** directly through Qiwa, rather than waiting indefinitely for a reply that may never come. This step protects your timeline and formally puts the ball in the employer's court.
 
---
 
## Bottom Line
 
Mutual agreement termination is, in itself, a flexible and fair tool for both sides — it only becomes risky when it's handled on trust alone, without documentation. The golden rule is simple: **nothing is truly agreed until it's written down and confirmed on Qiwa.**
